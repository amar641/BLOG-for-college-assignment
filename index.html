Overview

The Greatest Threat to Human Health in the 21st Century

In 2009, the Lancet Commission on Health and Climate Change declared climate change "the greatest threat to global health of the 21st century." By the end of 2023, the World Health Organization (WHO) had reinforced this assessment — identifying climate change as the single most significant threat to human health globally (WHO, 2024; Medical Science Monitor, 2024).

The Earth's atmospheric temperature has increased by approximately 1.5°C over the last century, driven primarily by the greenhouse effect — the trapping of long-wave infrared radiation by CO₂, methane, and nitrous oxide (Valentová & Bostik, 2021). Concentrations of CO₂ have risen 47%, methane by 156%, and nitrous oxide by 23% since the Industrial Revolution (Zhao et al., 2022).

The 2023 Lancet Countdown Report concluded that "there has been little progress in protecting individuals from the adverse health effects of climate change" — and that the inequalities in those effects are deepening, with developing nations bearing the heaviest burden from crises they did little to create.

"Environmental changes due to climate change are so huge that they threaten to negate all positive public health advances made through scientific progress." — Dr. Y.J. Anupama, Nanjappa Hospital, Karnataka (2023)

In 2022, global temperatures reached their highest point in over 100,000 years. That same year, global investment in fossil fuels paradoxically increased by 10%, exceeding USD 1 trillion — demonstrating the profound disconnect between scientific evidence and economic policy.

Direct & Indirect Impacts

How Global Warming Harms Human Health

Research across 26 peer-reviewed papers consistently identifies multiple distinct pathways through which rising temperatures damage human health — from direct physiological stress to cascading effects on food, water, and disease ecology.

🌡️
Direct Impact

Heat Stress & Heatwaves

Rising temperatures increase the frequency, intensity, and duration of heatwaves. The 2003 European heatwave caused over 70,000 deaths; the 2010 Russian heatwave killed over 11,000. Global warming has directly increased such events. Above 27°C, daily mortality increases more rapidly per degree rise than below it.

Heat-related illness spectrum includes: heat cramps, heat syncope, heat exhaustion, heatstroke, and cardiovascular/respiratory collapse. Vulnerable groups include the elderly (65+), infants, outdoor workers, and those with pre-existing heart or lung conditions.

Key Finding (Lancet 2023): People over 65 are now exposed to twice as many heatwave days as they were in 1986–2005. Heat-related deaths in this group rose by 85% compared to 1990–2000.
❤️
Cardiovascular

Heart Disease & Failure

Both temperature extremes — cold spells and heatwaves — are independently associated with increased cardiovascular morbidity and mortality. High ambient temperatures increase heart rate and blood pressure, trigger arrhythmias, and worsen fluid balance in patients with existing heart failure (Kakaei Dolatabadi et al., 2021).

Air pollution, which co-intensifies with heat events, exacerbates atherosclerosis and thrombosis risk. Ozone and fine particulate matter (PM₂.₅) directly impair cardiac function through inflammatory pathways.

Key Finding: High air temperature is associated with decreased heart rate variability, increasing risk of sudden cardiac arrest. Climate change is expected to increase incidence and mortality rates due to heart disease significantly.
🫁
Respiratory

Lung & Airway Diseases

Global warming worsens air quality through increased ground-level ozone formation, higher pollen concentrations, and more frequent wildfires producing fine particulate matter. These conditions dramatically worsen asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and allergic rhinitis (Anupama, 2023).

Higher CO₂ levels also stimulate longer and heavier pollen seasons, expanding the range of allergenic plants. Research shows a co-effect of high temperature and air pollution that amplifies asthma hospitalization rates by 2–3× during heatwave-pollution events.

Key Finding: Increased outdoor temperatures in COPD and anemia patients measurably reduce cerebral blood flow and respiratory function — a particularly dangerous combination in vulnerable populations.
🧠
Neurological

Brain & Nervous System

Emerging research identifies direct neurological impacts of climate change. Extreme heat impairs cognitive function, reduces attention and decision-making capacity, and increases the risk of delirium and stroke in elderly populations. Heat-related cerebrovascular events are rising in frequency.

Air pollutants associated with higher temperatures, including ozone and particulate matter, have been linked to accelerated neurodegeneration and increased risk of Alzheimer's disease and Parkinson's disease through neuroinflammatory pathways.

Key Finding: Elevated outdoor temperatures cause measurable reductions in cerebral blood flow rate, particularly in patients with pre-existing respiratory or circulatory conditions, increasing stroke risk.
🌾
Food Security

Malnutrition & Food Insecurity

Climate change threatens all fundamental pillars of food security: availability, access, utilization, and stability. Higher temperatures reduce crop yields by accelerating plant development, impairing photosynthesis, and increasing pest and disease pressure. Droughts and floods further disrupt agricultural systems.

Malnutrition, particularly in children under 5 in tropical and sub-Saharan Africa, is being significantly worsened by erratic rainfall patterns, soil degradation, and desertification linked to global warming (Farooqi et al., 2024).

Key Finding (Lancet 2023): In 2021, heatwave and drought frequency was associated with 127 million more people facing moderate-to-severe food insecurity compared to 1981–2010 baseline.
🫘
Renal Health

Kidney Disease & Stones

Heat stress causes excessive sweating and dehydration, reducing kidney perfusion and increasing concentration of stone-forming minerals in urine. Multiple studies link rising ambient temperatures to increased rates of urolithiasis (kidney stones), acute kidney injury (AKI), and chronic kidney disease of non-traditional origin (CKDnT).

Agricultural workers in tropical regions, who face the dual burden of heat and heavy physical labor with limited access to clean water, show particularly alarming rates of CKDnT in Central America, India, and Sri Lanka — a pattern directly linked to occupational heat exposure.

Key Finding: Global warming is linked to heat stress causing kidney stones, heat cramps, heat syncope, and heat exhaustion — a spectrum of conditions set to expand significantly with each degree of warming.
💧
Water Security

Water Scarcity & Contamination

Climate change disrupts the global water cycle through irregular precipitation, accelerated glacial melt, and increased evapotranspiration. Warmer temperatures raise water temperatures in rivers, lakes, and reservoirs, accelerating the growth of harmful algal blooms and bacterial pathogens (Rossati, 2017).

Post-flood environments consistently show increased outbreaks of leptospirosis, campylobacter, and cryptosporidiosis. Warmer water temperatures directly accelerate the transmission of waterborne pathogens including Vibrio bacteria, which causes cholera and severe gastrointestinal infections.

Key Finding: Vibrio pathogen habitat ranges are expanding by 17–25% as ocean surface temperatures rise, raising the risk of cholera and severe seafood-borne infections in newly affected coastal regions.
☀️
UV & Skin

Skin Cancer & UV Damage

Ozone depletion — linked to the same industrial emissions driving global warming — increases UV-B radiation reaching Earth's surface. This elevates the incidence of non-melanocytic skin cancers (NMSC), including basal cell carcinoma (BCC) and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), as well as melanoma and cataracts.

Increased UV exposure also suppresses immune function (immunosuppression), potentially accelerating the reactivation of latent viruses including herpes simplex and reducing vaccine efficacy in high-UV exposure populations.

Key Finding: Ozone depletion increases UV-B radiation causing skin cancers, cataracts, and decreased immune system function, creating additional vulnerability to both infectious and non-communicable diseases.
Vector-Borne & Waterborne

Infectious Disease: A Shifting Global Map

Climate change is fundamentally altering the geography, seasonality, and intensity of infectious diseases. Warmer temperatures expand the range of mosquitoes, ticks, and other vectors into previously inhospitable regions, while warmer oceans accelerate waterborne pathogen growth (Rossati, 2017; Kurane, 2010).

🦟

Dengue Fever

Warmer temperatures accelerate the Aedes mosquito's breeding cycle, shorten the virus incubation period inside the insect, and extend the mosquito's active season. Dengue is now spreading into subtropical and temperate regions including southern Europe and North America. The transmission potential for dengue is projected to increase substantially even at 1.5°C warming.

High & Rising Risk
🦠

Malaria

Malaria-carrying Anopheles mosquitoes are expanding into higher altitudes and latitudes as temperatures warm. East African highland regions previously too cold for transmission are now experiencing outbreaks. Even small temperature increases (1–2°C) can dramatically change the geographic boundary of malaria risk, exposing hundreds of millions of additional people.

Geographically Expanding
💧

Cholera & Vibriosis

Vibrio cholerae thrives in warm, brackish coastal waters. Rising sea surface temperatures and ocean acidification create conditions ideal for pathogen blooms. Flooding — increasingly frequent due to climate change — contaminate water supplies with fecal matter, amplifying cholera and other diarrhoeal outbreaks in low-income countries with inadequate sanitation infrastructure.

Flood-Amplified Risk
🕷️

Tick-Borne Diseases

Ixodes ticks carrying Lyme disease, tick-borne encephalitis, and babesiosis are expanding into previously tick-free boreal forests in Scandinavia, Canada, and Russia. Warmer winters reduce tick die-off, while earlier springs extend the active season. Tick density and distribution have increased in both geographic area and altitude.

Psychological Impact

The Mental Health Crisis Hidden in Climate Change

While physical health impacts receive greater attention, climate change poses serious and underappreciated threats to mental health. Research by Fritze et al. (2008) identifies three interconnected pathways through which climate change harms psychological wellbeing — from acute trauma to chronic existential anxiety.

⚡

Direct Trauma

Extreme weather events — floods, wildfires, hurricanes — cause acute PTSD, acute stress disorder, depression, and grief. Disaster survivors face disruption of community, home, and livelihood. Studies show elevated PTSD rates of 30–40% among flood survivors in developed countries and higher in developing nations with weaker mental health infrastructure.

🏘️

Social Disruption

Climate change undermines social determinants of mental health: stable housing, food security, income, community cohesion, and cultural identity. Agricultural communities facing crop failure and rural-to-urban displacement show elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and suicide. Indigenous communities losing ancestral lands face a unique form of ecological grief.

🌐

Eco-Anxiety

Growing awareness of climate change's scale and perceived helplessness in the face of global inaction creates chronic psychological distress, particularly in younger generations. Climate grief, eco-anxiety, and solastalgia (grief over environmental change) are increasingly recognized as clinically significant mental health challenges in populations worldwide.

"Direct impacts of climate change such as extreme weather events will have significant mental health implications; the most severe consequences are being felt by disadvantaged communities." — Fritze, Blashki, Burke & Wiseman (2008) · International Journal of Mental Health Systems
Evidence of Harm

Climate-Linked Mortality: A Growing Record

There is robust epidemiological evidence linking rising temperatures to increased all-cause mortality. The relationship is non-linear: at temperatures above 27°C, the daily mortality rate increases more rapidly per degree compared to temperatures below this threshold (Early Human Development, 2021).

Heat and cold-related deaths are chronically under-coded in mortality statistics — attributed instead to cardiovascular or respiratory causes that were precipitated by temperature extremes. This means the true burden of climate-related deaths is significantly higher than official counts suggest.

2003 — Europe
The summer heatwave kills over 70,000 people across Western Europe — a preview of what routine summers may look like under 2–3°C warming scenarios.
2010 — Russia
A prolonged heatwave causes over 11,000 deaths in Moscow and surrounding areas, with agricultural fires blanketing cities in toxic smoke, compounding respiratory mortality.
1990–2022 — Global
Heat-related deaths in people over 65 increased by 85% compared to 1990–2000, substantially exceeding the 38% increase that population aging alone would have caused.
2023 — Record Year
The WHO reports 2023 as the hottest year in over 100,000 years. Extreme heat events caused unprecedented mortality across South Asia, Southern Europe, and North America.
2030 — Projected
A multi-country study estimates climate change will be responsible for 700,000 additional deaths per year by 2030 under current emissions trajectories (Zhao et al., 2022).

Health Equity

Inequality in the Face of Climate Change

Climate change does not harm all populations equally. The countries that contribute least to greenhouse gas emissions typically face the greatest health burdens. Multiple papers in this review emphasize that health inequalities are being systematically deepened by climate change, with devastating consequences for developing nations, indigenous communities, and socially marginalized groups.

🌍

Developing Nations

Tropical and sub-Saharan African nations bear the greatest infectious disease burden from rising temperatures, yet contribute a fraction of global emissions. Ghana study (Odonkor & Sallar, 2020): 83.4% of respondents reported climate change impacts on their health, yet infrastructure to respond remains severely limited.

🧒

Children & Future Generations

Children face not only present-day climate health impacts but also the full weight of future warming locked in by current emissions. Hansen et al. (2013) argue that continued high fossil fuel emissions "would be an act of extraordinary witting intergenerational injustice." Children's developing bodies are more vulnerable to heat, malnutrition, and air pollution.

🏙️

Urban Heat Island Residents

Urban areas experience 2–5°C higher temperatures than surrounding rural areas due to heat island effects — concrete, asphalt, and reduced vegetation trap heat. Low-income urban residents, without access to air conditioning or green spaces, face compounded heat exposure. Urban planning is increasingly recognized as a critical public health intervention.

The Climate Justice Paradox

The 2024 Medical Science Monitor review concluded: "inequalities in the effects of climate change, including the impact on health, are increasing." The nations that benefit economically from fossil fuel extraction are predominantly wealthy and have strong health systems to buffer climate impacts. The nations suffering the worst health consequences are predominantly poor, with under-resourced healthcare systems unable to cope with the rising burden.

Economic Cost of Inaction

The Lancet Countdown 2023 reports that economic losses from extreme-weather events increased by 23% between 2010–2014 and 2018–2022, reaching USD $264 billion in 2022 alone. Heat exposure led to global potential income losses of USD $863 billion in labour productivity. Meanwhile, the 20 wealthiest nations invested a collective $489 billion annually in fossil fuels — the very source of these costs.

Responses & Solutions

Evidence-Based Pathways to Protect Health

Research across these 26 papers converges on a clear message: both mitigation (reducing emissions) and adaptation (adjusting to changes already locked in) are essential. Health protection must be central to climate policy — not an afterthought.

01

Rapid Decarbonization

Hansen et al. (2013) calculate that limiting cumulative industrial-era fossil fuel emissions to less than 500 GtC is necessary to keep climate within the Holocene range to which human civilization is adapted. This requires a rising price on carbon emissions and phase-down of conventional and unconventional fossil fuels. Every fraction of a degree avoided directly reduces mortality.

02

Renewable Energy Transition

Transitioning from fossil fuels to solar, wind, and other clean energy sources reduces CO₂ emissions while simultaneously delivering local air quality benefits — reducing respiratory and cardiovascular disease through lower particulate matter and ozone. Renewable energy is now the fastest-growing energy source globally, though pace remains insufficient to meet climate targets.

03

Health System Adaptation

Medical preparedness for climate change includes heat-health early warning systems, expanded surveillance for emerging infectious diseases, climate-resilient healthcare infrastructure, and training healthcare workers to recognize climate-attributable conditions. The 2024 Medical Science Monitor review emphasizes developing prevention and adaptation programs as a core health sector responsibility.

04

Afforestation & Urban Greening

Forests absorb CO₂ and provide critical co-benefits for human health: urban trees reduce heat island temperatures by 2–8°C, improve air quality by filtering particulates, and provide psychological benefits. Reforestation and afforestation programs — combined with ending deforestation — can store an estimated 100 GtC in biosphere and soil according to Hansen et al. (2013).

05

Disease Surveillance & Early Warning

Expanding real-time surveillance for vector-borne diseases, monitoring tick and mosquito range expansion, and developing climate-informed infectious disease forecasting systems are essential public health tools. Kurane (2010) emphasizes that adaptation measures are the most practical immediate action, since global warming cannot be halted for decades even with aggressive mitigation.

06

Public Education & Policy

A Ghana study (Odonkor & Sallar, 2020) found that while 84.4% of respondents understood global warming, public health education strategies lag far behind the need. Television and social media reach the widest audiences. Governments must develop sustained communication strategies translating scientific evidence into actionable public health guidance on heat safety, food hygiene, water quality, and mental health support.

Conclusion

The Imperative for a Health-Centred Response

The 26 peer-reviewed papers synthesized in this report paint an unambiguous picture: global warming is already causing measurable, significant harm to human health across every organ system, every age group, and every continent. From the 85% rise in heat-related deaths among the elderly, to expanding dengue fever ranges, to deepening food insecurity affecting 127 million additional people — the evidence is overwhelming and growing.

The 2023 Lancet Countdown report's central message — "the imperative for a health-centred response in a world facing irreversible harms" — must be the lens through which all climate policy is evaluated. The health costs of inaction already exceed the economic costs of aggressive mitigation. The window for preventing the worst outcomes is narrowing rapidly.
